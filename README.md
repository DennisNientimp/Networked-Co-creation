# Implementation-of-Community-Energy-by-Combining-Social-Network-Analysis-and-Co-creation
These are the anonymous data files and R scripts used for a research project that promoted co-creation through network methods with two active CEIs. The doi to the publicationwill be added at a later point

Here the abstract: 
European community energy initiatives (CEIs) struggle with low participation numbers, undermining sustainability goals and exacerbating socioeconomic divides. While policy makers granted subsidies and CEI literature boomed, research indicates that translating findings into practice remains difficult. This can be ascribed to a lack of deep stakeholder engagement in policy and research and the underutilization of social structural characteristics in CEI implementation. Both relate to the neglect of modern conceptions of governance as regulation of social behavior through citizen networks. Moreover, the lack of engagement leaves stakeholders with little agency and contradicts the proposed European “energy citizen” framework.
Therefore, we combine co-creation and social network analysis in four co-creation sessions with two CEIs acting as co-researchers. We utilized insights from 9 earlier case studies, initiators mapped their local networks and we discussed factors pertinent to the implementation of CEIs to tackle the collectively devised goal of developing targeted mobilization strategies. Further, survey and network data were collected in the respective communities and collectively analyzed. 
Qualitative data and session evaluations revealed that the initiators learned, derived research insights, and enjoyed the sessions. The outcomes indicate that 1) a networked co-creation approach enables deep stakeholder engagement in research that facilitates trust, knowledge exchange, and mutual learning and that 2) engaging initiators provides the necessary contextual insights to facilitate CEI implementation. The project outcomes point towards the need for more stakeholder engagement in policy implementation and research and highlights the contextual nature of CEIs, implying that a “one size fits all” approach is counterproductive.
